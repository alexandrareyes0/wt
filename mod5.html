Types Of Foundations

This is the fifth module. This module introduces the different categories of offshore wind foundations then explains each type of foundation along with its respective advantages and disadvantages.

Main Focus

The main focus of this module is to be familiar with the functions of each foundation, understand when each should be implemented, and be able to identify the foundation based on an image.

Goal

The goal of this section is to understand why different foundations are used depending on factors such as distance from shore, depth, and others.

Loaction

The location of offshore wind turbines varies and is dependent on several facts. For example, turbines need to be located above the crest levels of the highest waves and in depths that are suitable for the foundation type, and based on OWFs on the continental shelf, they are located about 10km off the coast in water depths of 10m. In addition, typically offshore wind farms need to be located near onshore transmission substations.

Fixed Foundations

Fixed foundations for offshore wind turbines are installed directly into the seabed and are commonly used in relatively shallow waters. Different types of fixed-foundations are used depending on the depth of the water they are to be installed in. These foundations are buried deep into the seabed in order to support the tower of the turbine.

Gravity Base

A concrete structure consisting of sand, rock, and iron ore are used to fill the base. The weight of this foundation is typically over 1,000 tons and has a diameter of about 15 m. Then a concrete or steel shaft connects the transition piece to the tower. This foundation is suitable for shallow depths between 0-30 m.

Advantages

  1. 1. Uses cheaper materials such as concrete and steel
  2. 2. Technology is proven because it was borrowed from oil & gas
  3. 3. Tugboats can move GBFs assembled onshore into place which reduces costs and risks

Monopile

Most commonly used foundation. Consists of a steel tube buried about 30m into the seabed. It has a diameter ranging from 3 m - 8m, a height of 50-60m, and a weight of 500-800 tons. These foundations are suitable for shallow depths between 0-40m.

Advantages

  1. 1. Works well in sand and gravel

  2. 2. Simple design and quick installation

  3. 3. Adaptable design: both shallow and deeper installation of various sizes is possible

Disadvantages

  1. 1. Cost and risk increase as monopile size increases

  2. 2. Installation noise can negatively impact marine life

  3. 3. Requires extra scour protection around its base

Tripod

Three legged, lightweight structure that is connected to a single cylindrical column which supports the turbine tower. Looks like a monopile from above the waves. Tripod is used in transitional depths ranging from 10-80m

Advantages

  1. 1. Seabed does not need advanced preparation

  2. 2. Works well in stiff clay or dense sand. Can also be used in soft sand/soil

  3. 3. Economical choice for depths 45m or greater

Disadvantages

  1. 1. Construction and maintenance costs can be higher than other foundations

  2. 2. Not suited for areas with several large boulders

  3. 3. Not suited for depths less than 6-7 meters

Jacket

Lattice structure with three to four cylindrical legs, that are anchored into the seabed, with diagonal struts. The jacket foundation is suitable for transitional depths ranging from 5m-80m.

Advantages


  1. 1. Can be installed using piles or suction caissons

  2. 2. A large lattice surface area can act as an artificial reef for marine life

  3. 3. Economical and can be moved by barge

Disadvantages


  1. 1. May allow invasive species

  2. 2. History of grout joint issues

  3. 3. Installation noise can be damaging to marine life

Floating Foundations

Floating foundations, unlike fixed foundations, are not drilled directly into the seabed. Instead, they are kept in place by long cables that are attached the the sea floor. This allows for turbines with these types of foundations to be deployed much farther (up to 200-300m).

Pros & Cons of Floating Foundations

Floating foundations have benefits that fixed foundations do not, but there is a discrepency between the technology of floating and fixed foundations being that there is still a great deal of research and innovation required until floating foundations can begin to be more commonly deployed in offshore wind farms globally.

Advantages

  1. 1. Increases the possible distance of OWFs from shore by over 200m and allows access to 58% of wind resources that fixed foundations cannot reach

  2. 2. Turbines and bases can be assembled onshore

  3. 3. Minimizes risks to birds as well as visual impacts from shore due to distance

Disadvantages

  1. 1. The cables that are needed to anchor platforms require constant inspection and maintenance

  2. 2. Anchors and cables can disrupt sealife

  3. 3. Increased possibility of invasive species
