Life Cycle

This is the sixth module. This module will guide you through each phase of an offshore wind farm's life and include an explanation of each event and why it is necessary.

Main Focus

The main focus of this module is to be familiar with the functions of each foundation, understand when each should be implemented, and be able to identify the foundation based on an image.

Goal

The goal of this section is to understand why different foundations are used depending on factors such as distance from shore, depth, and others.

Overview

An offshore wind farm requires several prior steps before it can be opened for power generation. Before installation can commence, a series of surveys and studies are done at the site to ensure the introduction of wind turbines is feasible and non disruptive to inhabitants of the surrounding area (including marine life, birds, and people).

Once pre construction surveys are completed, installation can begin. This process includes turbine and substation installation as well as cable laying operations.

After construction, the commission phase begins. This consists of a series of tests to ensure the integrity and functionality of the wind farm. Once this phase is complete, the wind farm is handed off to an operations and maintenance team who are able to repair any issues.

An offshore wind farm has an average lifecycle of about 25 years. After its life cycle has been completed, there are several options for the farm. These include extending its operational life, repowering, or a full decommission of the wind farm.

Pre Construction

The conditions of the proposed OWF area are unknown until a series of surveys are done. This includes geophysical and geotechnical surveys.

Geophysical surveys support studies of engineering, environmental, and marine archaeology which are essential to the offshore wind development process. This survey shows any geological constraints or hazards.

Geotechnical surveys include sampling and testing the seabed characteristics. This is important for placement of turbines, substations, and foundations.

These environmental surveys require at least 15-20 weeks per project, but can be extended up to several years (2-5).

Installation

After the surveying phase is completed, the installation phase can commence. Construction typically takes one to two years. The process includes:

1. Port:
Components of the turbine are pre assembled

2a. Foundation Installation:
Monopile- monopiles are the most common foundation type. They are installed by being driven into the seabed using large pile hammers (approx. 2-3 days per monopile)

2b. Foundation Installation:
Jacket- lowered into place by floating or jack up vessels then placed over pin piles and grouted
Floating- completely pre assembled onshore then towed to site by tugs

3. Transition Piece:
Only applicable to monopiles: Connects monopile foundation to turbine tower
Other foundation types already have transition pieces or do not require them

4. Turbine Installation:
Turbine towers are typically preassembled onshore and then transported to the site where the nacelle and blades are installed

5. Substation Installation:
Preassembled onshore and installed at once as a single unit using a jack-up vessel or vessel with heavy lifting capabilities

6. Cable Laying Operations:
Array and export cables are installed by specialized cable-lay vessels and are buried under the seabed by either trenching and burial or rock dumping

6.a. Cable pull-in:
Array cable- pulling cable into turbine or substation foundations
Export cable- pulling cable to shore and substations

6.b. Electrical Testing and Termination:
Tests and proves cable’s integrity as well as the connection between the offshore cable and the turbines, substation, and onshore cables

Post Construction

Commissioning includes a series of inspections and tests, different from previous tests done in prior phases, which include visual inspection, electrical testing, operation testing, sensors testing, and more.

Once construction is complete, a team of approximately 2 people for every 20-30 wind turbines are assigned as a designated operations and maintenance crew for the OWF and provide 24/7, 365 service to any unexpected hindrances

Decommissioning

Offshore wind farms have an average life span of 25 years. At the end of its lifecycle, there are three possible outcomes for the farm. These include: life extension, repowering, or a full decommission.

Life Extension:
an assessment of the condition is done on the OWF to determine the amount of maintenance that would be required to significantly extend its operational life. If the costs of repairs surpasses the potential value of electricity it can produce, its life will not be extended

Repower:
consists of replacing existing turbines with newer and larger ones. In addition foundations and array cables will be updated as well in order to improve its efficiency.

Full Decommission:
when cost of maintenance or repowering will not be economically beneficial. Turbines are fully dismantled and removed. Method of removal is dependent on foundation type. 85% of the turbine can be recycled.
