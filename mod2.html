How It Works

This is the second module. After learning the basic attributes of an offshore wind farm, we will delve deeper into their infrastructure and operations!

Main Focus

-Understand how offshore wind farms generate and distribute power
-Gain knowledge on the parts of a wind turbine and how they function
-Understand the purpose of offshore substations

Goal

The goal of this section is to have a basic understanding of offshore wind before diving deeper into the specifics of its components and operations

Overview

Offshore wind farms consist of several wind turbines that are connected to offshore substations by array cables and finally sent to the shore by export cables. Wind turbines are giant structures that transform the wind's kinetic energy into electrical energy. This is passed to the offshore substation, through cables, where the voltage is increased. From the substation, it can be sent onshore to a transmission network where it can then go on to provide power to the community.

Wind Turbines

Size

Offshore wind turbines are massive structures and continue to increase in size as innovation progresses. Their size allows them to capture more wind power and generate more electricity than a smaller turbine would be able to.

For comparison, the Statue of Liberty is 350 ft tall while wind turbines located in the Block Island Offshore Wind Project in Rhode Island are 590 ft tall, and the largest offshore wind turbine, the GE Haliade-X, is 853 ft tall.

Parts

Wind Turbines are comprised of a series of parts with different vital functions. These include:

Hub

Supports the blades and holds the pitch system (optimizes blade angle and rotation speed)

Blades

Most wind turbines have 3 blades. Wind blows over the blades which allows them to lift and rotate

Rotor

Rotating part of the turbine. Consists of the three blades and the hub

Nacelle

Located on top of the tower and is connected to the rotor. Holds the components that convert mechanical energy to electrical energy

Tower

Made of tubular steel or steel lattice and supports the nacelle, hub, and blades

Foundation

Secures tower to sea floor. Different foundations include monopile, jacket, and gravity based foundations

How It Works

The wind blows across the blade causing air pressure to increase on one side while it decreases on the other. This creates lift and drag which ultimately causes the rotor to spin.

Then, the rotor connects to the generator and several gears that speed up rotation and make electricity.

The electricity generated within a turbine transformer is then sent, through array cables, to a collection system called an offshore substation. There, the voltage is increased and transferred onshore.

Offshore Substation

The offshore substation is a vital part of the wind farm. An offshore wind farm can have one or more, depending on its capacity. These substations weigh between 10,000 to 22,000 tons and consist of a topside deck and are installed with either monopile or jacket foundations. The wind turbines transfer the generated electricity to the offshore substation where its voltage is increased and stabilized. A maximized voltage ensures minimal electrical losses as it is being sent ashore. The power is then transmitted through cables buried deep within the seabed called export cables. Export cables connect the offshore substation to its onshore point of connection.
