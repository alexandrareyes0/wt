Main Focus

The main focus of this module is to see the advancements of offshore wind throughout the last 50 years and be aware of the presence of offshore wind developments in the near future within the United States and other states such as New Jersey

Goal

The goal of this section is to have an awareness of offshore wind's development and its future in the US

Wind History

Wind energy has been a vital tool in the lives of all people as early as 5000 BC. Wind was used as a propellant for boats, and later used for water pumps used in farms. As time went on and technology advanced, wind power was spread worldwide and used for a variety of purposes such as food production in the Middle East, to drain lakes in the Netherlands, and to grind grain in America. Wind is an unlimited resources that has been used for centuries in a multitude of ways, and now offshore wind farms can be used to produce enough electricity to power up to 80% of United States residents along the coast. This advancement of wind technology has been used in several other nations and has proven to be an economic and eco friendly form of power production.

A Timeline of Offshore Wind

1970

Gas prices skyrocket, so interest & research in wind energy is renewed

1978

President Carter signs Public Regulatory Policies Act of 1978 (requires companies to buy a certain amount of renewable energy, e.g. wind)

1980

First large scale (/utility scale) wind mills are installed in California (onshore)

1981

“The Viterna Method” was created by Larry Viterna and Bob Corrigan, which is a method used to predict the performance of wind turbines. This method is still used today and improves the efficiency and output of current wind turbines

1991

The first offshore wind farm was constructed in shallow water off the coast of Denmark. Dong Energy, now Orsted, was responsible for the 11 turbine wind farm, Vindeby. It totaled 5 MW in capacity and was able to power 2,200 Danish households

1992

Energy Act is signed by President Bush which authorizes a production tax credit of 1.5 cents per kWh of wind-generated power and puts a focus back on to renewable energy

1993

National Wind Technology Center is created

2001

First large scale wind farm, Middelgrunden, is opened off of Copenhagen. Consisted of 20 wind turbines and made up 3% of Copenhagen’s total power consumption (UK)

2011

a). The world’s first floating wind turbine, the Hywind, begins operation in the North Sea (UK)

b). The US Department of Energy and the Department of Interior announce their offshore wind strategy to reduce the cost of energy

2012

The world’s largest offshore wind farm opens off the coast of Cumbria, England. It has a capacity of 367 MW (UK)

2013

DOE partners with the University of Maine in a project to deploy a floating wind turbine that is connected to the grid

2016

the first American offshore wind farm opens in Rhode Island: Block Island Wind Farm (30 MW)

2017

Hywind Scotland: The world’s first floating wind farm is commissioned (UK)

2018

a). DOE reports that the US has 25, 434 MW of offshore wind energy in pipeline. Projects are in development in New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, and Virginia

b). January 31, “Governor Phil Murphy Signed Executive Order #8 which directs all New Jersey State Agencies with responsibilities under the OWEDA to fully implement the act in order to meet a goal of obtaining 3,500 MW from offshore wind by the year 2030.” (nj.gov)

2019

a). June 21- “ the NJBPU announced that Ørsted's Ocean Wind 1 Project was awarded a solicitation for 1,100 MW offshore wind project. This 1,100 MW project has the potential to power nearly half a million homes in New Jersey.”

b.) November 19- Governor Murphy signed Executive Order #9. “raised New Jersey's offshore wind goal from 3,500 megawatts by 2030 to 7,500 megawatts by 2035 - more than double the initial goal. This new goal will deliver clean, renewable energy generation needed to meet the State's goals of 50% renewable energy by 2030 and 100% clean energy by 2050.” (nj.gov)

The United State's Plan

Under the Biden-Harris Administration, the President set a goal of achieving 30 gigawatts of offshore wind energy by 2030. Offshore wind turbines are installed directly into the sea floor in shallow waters, like off the East Coast, but to capture more wind energy offshore wind farms need to be deployed in deeper waters, such as off the West Coast. Floating foundations are necessary for this to be possible, hence why the Department of Interior announced a new goal of 15 GW of floating offshore wind capacity installed by 2035 through the Energy Earthshot ™ Program. To create the Floating Offshore Wind Shot. The Wind Shot is to reduce costs of floating base technologies by 70% to $45 per mWh by 2035.

NJ's Plan

Governor Phil Murphy has set a goal of achieving 7.5 GW of offshore wind energy by 2035 as a part of the state’s goal of reaching 100% clean energy by 2050. This would mean that New Jersey would generate 30% of offshore wind energy off of the East Coast. New Jersey’s geological location in the middle of the Eastern Seaboard allows for a great amount of offshore wind capacity as well as the potential to house a hub for manufacturing and staging wind turbines for shipment throughout the OCS.

New Jersey awards offshore wind solicitations every two years with three projects underway: Orsted’s Ocean Wind I (1,100 MW capacity), EDF/Shell’s Atlantic Shores Offshore Wind, and Orsted’s Ocean Wind II (2,568 MW combined capacity). The state will also fund scholarships, workforce development, and several other programs to help overburdened communities.

New Jersey is constructing the first purpose-built offshore wind marshaling port in the United States called the New Jersey Wind Port. Manufacturing of wind turbine components as well as marshaling and staging will occur here. In addition to the NJWP, the state is constructing the Port of Paulsboro where monopile foundations will be manufactured.

Finally, New Jersey has created a supply chain within the offshore wind industry in order to allow established companies to partner with/purchase from New Jersey-based businesses. This will aid in economic growth within the state.
